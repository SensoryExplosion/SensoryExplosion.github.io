Pet insurance reimagined

Designing a native insurance experience shaped by behavioural science, clearer product storytelling, and simpler legal consent.

The case for change

From product ownership to design

I originally worked on the product as a Product Manager, representing my team during a research project with a behavioral science research firm. Together, we examined the academic literature, insurance best practices, and our existing customer journeys to understand where and why customers were dropping off. In this project, I step into the role of a designer to translate those research findings into a native experience.

What the research revealed

Insurance asks people to make an abstract, emotionally charged decision about an uncertain future. Their value is difficult to perceive before something goes wrong, while dense product details and legal language increase the cognitive load at the moment of decision-making. Trust, emotional attachment, perceived risks, and social influences therefore influence decisions just as much as coverage and premiums.

The focus of the redesign is to make an abstract product more tangible and relatable while reducing cognitive load, without compromising transparency and legal requirements.

03 / Design response

Three research findings shaped the redesign.

Each design move responds directly to a behavioural finding from the research.

01

Framing + loss aversion

Frame the risk, then offer reassurance

Start with a relatable vet-bill risk, then position protection as peace of mind.

02

Perceived value

Make value human, not only functional

Show care, choice, and support before price, limits, and exclusions.

03

Chunking + Spark Effect

Reduce effort at every step

Break information and forms into focused, manageable actions.

Barking up the right tree

Frame the insurance need around concrete risks before presenting coverage. This makes the product feel relevant before users start comparing features.

Value before variables

Explain the value of coverage before leading with limits and prices. The goal is to make the benefits understandable before the user evaluates cost.

The power of stories

Use testimonials as a bridge from abstract insurance language to real outcomes, showing how the product helps when a pet owner needs support.

Paw after paw

Splitting pet details across focused steps reduces cognitive load and creates a smoother, more manageable flow.

Clear copy, informed choices

Explain the purpose of each document with friendly copy and simple structure, so users can understand what they are agreeing to before they continue.

From toggle-maze to a tap

Replace multiple toggles with one confirmation cell for a take-it-or-leave-it consent scenario, reducing interaction cost without hiding the required terms.

Clearer, warmer, easier to act on

This case study should show how thoughtful UX can make even regulated insurance products feel clearer, warmer, and easier to act on.
